AI Models

The Machine Learning Brains

We don't trust just one "expert." Our system runs a competition between four distinct mathematical brains.

Level: Basic Type: Classification

1. Logistic Regression

The foundation of automated decisioning. Despite its name, it's used for classification (Yes/No answers).

Simple Analogy

"Imagine drawing a straight line through a field of dots. Points on the left are rejections, points on the right are approvals. It's like finding the cleanest way to divide a group based on their stats."

Technical Detail: It uses the Sigmoid Function to map any real-valued number into a value between 0 and 1. If the score is > 0.5, the loan is approved. It weighs features like 'Income' versus 'Debt' linearly.

Level: Advanced Type: Ensemble

2. Random Forest

A "Wisdom of the Crowds" approach. It uses hundreds of individual Decision Trees to vote on the outcome.

Simple Analogy

"Think of it as asking 100 different loan officers a series of Yes/No questions. One might focus on income, another on credit age. We take the majority vote of the whole crowd to eliminate individual mistakes."

Technical Detail: Uses Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating) to reduce variance and prevent 'Overfitting' (memorizing data instead of learning). Highly robust against missing data.

Level: Advanced Type: Gradient Boosting

3. XGBoost

The current gold standard for spreadsheet-style data. It's like an elite team that learns from its own failures.

Simple Analogy

"Imagine a group of students taking a test. The second student focuses only on the questions the first student got wrong. The third student focuses on what the second got wrong. They get smarter every turn."

Technical Detail: Uses Gradient Descent to minimize error. It builds trees sequentially, where each new tree corrects the 'residuals' of the previous ones. It is incredibly efficient and fast.

Level: Expert Type: Deep Learning

4. Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)

A system inspired by the human brain. It finds hidden, non-linear patterns that humans might never see.

Simple Analogy

"Patterns within patterns. It doesn't just look at Income; it looks at how Income interacts with Credit Age and Debt in thousands of tiny, invisible ways, passing information through layers of digital 'neurons'."

Technical Detail: A 3-layer architecture (128 → 64 → 1) built with PyTorch. It uses ReLU activation functions to handle complex relationships and Dropout layers to maintain accuracy on new applicants.

Trust & Clarity

Explainable AI (XAI)

A "Yes" or "No" isn't enough. In bank regulation, we must explain why.

SHAP Values

We use SHapley Additive exPlanations. It's a method from game theory that assigns a 'contribution' score to every single feature.

Example: "This loan was rejected. High Loan Amount pulled the score down by 0.3, while Good Income pushed it up by 0.1, but not enough to offset the risk."

Fraud Detection

Using Isolation Forest, we identify 'mathematically weird' applications that don't fit the pattern of normal human behavior, flagging them for human review.
